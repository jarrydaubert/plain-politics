## Conclusion

The useful conclusion is not "make politics Gen Z". It is:

> Make politics feel navigable, personal, low-effort, and non-patronising.

Plain Politics should feel like a warm explainer tool with receipts: less institution, more orientation; less trust wording, more visible source behaviour; less politics-as-drama, more politics-as-life-admin with consequences.

## Evidence

1. Younger users often meet news socially, visually, and passively.

   Reuters Institute's 2026 Digital News Report says social media and video networks have overtaken both TV and news websites/apps globally as news access routes. It also says 52% of 18-24s globally now name social media, video networks, and AI chatbots as their main way of getting news.

   Source: <https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/digital-news-report/2026/dnr-executive-summary>

2. Proof still matters, but it should not feel like a lecture.

   Reuters Institute's 2025 Digital News Report says trusted news brands and official sources remain important places people use to check whether something is true or false, while younger users are more likely than older users to also use social media and AI chatbots.

   Source: <https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/digital-news-report/2025/dnr-executive-summary>

3. Information is social and context-shaped.

   The CHI 2023 paper "Practicing Information Sensibility" studied 35 Gen Z participants from across the US. Treat it as directional rather than universal. It found participants tended to encounter information rather than search for it, and their interpretation was shaped by social motivations, belonging, usefulness, and relevance.

   Source: <https://research.google/pubs/practicing-information-sensibility-how-gen-z-engages-with-online-information/>

4. Low-pressure participation matters.

   Ofcom's 2026 adults media research says UK social media use is becoming more passive and circumspect. Around half of adult social media users actively post, share, or comment, down from 61% in 2024 to 49%; concern about online posts causing future problems also increased.

   Source: <https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-adults/passive-social-media-use-ai-companionship-and-online-side-hustles-uk-adults-media-and-online-lives-revealed>

5. Younger audiences often reject the format politics comes in, not necessarily the subject.

   Reuters Institute's young audiences work describes 18-24s as more social-first, more creator-aware, and more likely to encounter news through TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and social feeds. Plain Politics should borrow useful behaviours - fast orientation, visual hierarchy, short summaries before depth, obvious next steps - without becoming a social feed.

   Source: <https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/understanding-young-news-audiences-time-rapid-change>

## Product Implications

1. Lead with orientation, not institutional structure.
   Use questions a person would actually ask: "Who represents me?", "Who voted for this?", "What does this word mean?", "Where did this come from?"

2. Keep v1 private and low-pressure.
   No forced accounts, no public comment prompts, no political identity profiling, no ideological scoring, no noisy badges, and no progress bars or unlock mechanics.

3. Show receipts through behaviour.
   Use "Open record", "Where this came from", "Last checked", "What changed", and "What is still unclear" near claims. Avoid repeating "trusted", "verified", "impartial", or "unbiased" as marketing copy.

4. Design for quick understanding before depth.
   Use visual summaries, "What it means" notes, inline glossary help, short explainers, and expandable evidence before long tables or methodology.

5. Use a warm explainer-tool aesthetic.
   Think civic cheat sheet with receipts: warm paper, notebook lines, highlighter marks, calm route cards, friendly icons, strong mobile layout, and grown-up typography. Avoid GOV-style service design, fake youth slang, memes, neon chaos, and political-game patterns.

6. Keep bigger life-first sections post-v1 unless they are actually wired.
   "Issues", "Do something", action templates, local council/mayor/devolved coverage, and policy timelines are strong directions, but they should not appear as fully available product areas until source coverage and routes exist.
